Chapter 5: Hazard Analysis, Future Work, and Conclusions
5.1 Hazard Analysis
5. 1.1 Landslide Dam

Movement of the Salmon Falls landslide has impinged on Salmon Falls Creek,
displacing the stream channel and forcing its former bed upward by more than 2 m on the
rising toe. The southern part of the toe has extended undemeath Salmon Falls Creek and
emerged on the western side of the channel. This activity has created two natural dams,
behind which two lakes have formed, the largest of which is 2 km long and estimated to
contain over 300,000 In3 of water (Ellis et al., 2004). The ﬂood hazards associated with
the failure of these dams may impact life and property located downstream.

Landslide dams can be classified with respect to their orientations with the valley
ﬂoor (Swanson et a1., 1986). The Salmon Falls landslide dam is a Type VI, in which one
or more failure surfaces extend under a stream or river valley and emerge on the opposite
side from the main landslide mass. These dams typically involve landslide activity with
slow basal sliding, and form lakes by raising the elevation of the streambed, changing the
local gradient of the stream. In these situations, the risk of downstream ﬂooding is
typically not great because the lakes are often small, outlet gradients are low, and the
stream-bed material that forms the dam may be difﬁcult to erode. The stream will often
continue to ﬂow over the dam debris, so that the likelihood of abrupt overtopping and
rapid incision of the dam is unlikely (Costa and Schuster, 1988).

A landslide dam in its natural state differs from a constructed embankment dam in
that it is made up of a heterogeneous mass of unconsolidated or poorly consolidated earth

materials. Natural landslide dams typically lack the characteristics that make

84

constructed dams stable. They have no engineered water barrier, ﬁlter zones to prevent
piping, drain zones to control pore pressures, or channelized spillways or other protected
outlets. Most landslide dams fail shortly after formation and often fail catastrophically,
causing major ﬂooding and loss of life (Costa and Schuster, 1988). However, some
landslide dams may last for several thousand years, depending on many factors, including
volume, size, shape, and sorting of blockage material; rates of seepage through the
blockage; and rates of sediment and water ﬂow into the newly formed lake (Costa and
Schuster, 1988; Schuster, 1993). Landslide dams consisting of large or cohesive particles
resist failure better than do dams containing large percentages of soils or soft rock (Costa
and Schuster, 1988).

The Salmon Falls landslide dam consists mostly of large basalt boulders ranging
in size from several centimeters up to ca. 1 meter in a matrix of ﬁne material (Fig. 31).
The ﬁnes are derived from the Glenns Ferry Formation sedimentary deposits and
Holocene alluvial and lacusttine deposits associated with the modern stream. These
deposits are now intermixed with the basalt boulders to form the bulk of the landslide
dam deposits.

Uplift of the old streambed exposes fine-grained alluvial deposits about a meter
thick on the east side of the current stream channel (Fig. 31). Water from the impounded
lake continues to ﬂow over the landslide dam, eroding away the streambed sediments and
ﬁne matrix material, leaving only the large boulders (Fig. 31). There has been some
minor erosion of the dam, probably as a result of the removal of the ﬁne matrix material

by the ﬂowing stream. The relatively small amount of downcutting into the darn

Furthermore, the ﬂow of Salmon Falls Creek is regulated by Salmon Falls dam,
located ca. 50 km upstream of the landslide. The dam was constructed in 1911 (Ellis et
al., 2004). USGS streamﬂow records since 1970 for Salmon Falls Creek near Hagerman,
ca. 10 km of the landslide, indicate that seasonal variations in daily—mean streamﬂow
generally vary between 0.85 and 8.5 m3/sec. The seasonal streamﬂows have remained
fairly constant over the 32-year period of record, and only rarely has the maximum daily-
mean streamﬂow exceeded 11.0 ma/sec. The maximum mean-daily streamﬂow during
the 32-year recording period was ca. 100 m3/sec in early 1984. Because of the regulating
effect of Salmon Falls Dam, there is a low probability of an abnormally high stream-ﬂow
event impacting the landslide dam (Ellis et al., 2004)

Two depth measurement transects of the large impounded lake conducted in
November 2001 by the BLM indicated that 50 m upstream of the dam the mean lake
depth was 4.5 m, and that about 400 m upstream of the dam the mean lake depth was 2.3
m. Based on the two stream transects, the BLM estimated the lake volume in November
2001, to be slightly greater than 260,000 m3. From late 2001 to early 2004, the toe of the
slide moved upward approximately 1 m, about 15 percent of the current dam height.
Assuming that the lake depth increased by a corresponding amount during this time
period, that the dam was not eroded, and that the lake did not widen signiﬁcantly in the
narrow canyon, the volume of the lake probably did not increase by more than about 15-
25 percent. It is therefore inferred that the volume of the large impounded lake is about

325,000 m3 (Ellis et al., 2004).

87

The toe of the landslide forms a steep cliff in some areas along Salmon Falls
Creek. The continued rise of the toe and westward movement of the slide may induce
smaller collapses, which could create more dams or debris ﬂows. The primary
hazard currently associated with the landslide is a possible breach of the landslide dam
that could cause ﬂooding and outburst debris ﬂows downstream from the landslide.

The worst-case scenario would be a sudden and catastrophic failure of the landslide dam,
resulting in a release of all or most of the impounded water over a short time period (Ellis
et al., 2004).

Given the current motion rate of the Salmon Falls landslide and pronounced dam
armoring, ﬂood hazards associated with dam failure appear to pose a minimal threat at
this time (Ellis et al., 2004; Thackray et al., 2004). However, should the landslide
catastrophically fail or accelerate markedly it is difﬁcult to predict whether the dam
would remain intact or if it too would catastrophically fail and induce other hazards such
as debris ﬂows. Furthermore, more rapid landslide movement could move the toe further
into the stream channel and build a higher dam, with consequent larger lake volumes and
more sizeable ﬂood hazards.

5. 1.2 Debris Flows

As previously mentioned, debris ﬂows are another potential hazard associated
with the Salmon Falls landslide. The westward and upward motion of the oversteepened
toe and inﬂux of landslide debris into Salmon Falls Creek could be sufﬁcient to induce

minor debris ﬂows. The landslide toe is composed of ﬁne-grained ﬂuvial and lacustrine

88

sediments that could be easily mobilized in a debris ﬂow. The extensive fracturing in
these ﬁne sediments produces further instability. Larger debris ﬂows could be induced
by catastrophic failure of the landslide and landslide dam. A dam breach of the
impounded lake combined with the sediment and debris delivery from the landslide toe
could produce large debris ﬂows.

Serious life and property loss downstream would likely be avoided because the
ﬂows would be constricted to the narrow Salmon Falls Creek Canyon and would have
time and space to dissipate naturally before reaching populated areas, located ca. 11 km
downstream. However, an irrigation pumping station that is located ca. 1 km
downstream from the landslide would likely be seriously damaged.

5. 1.3 Other LandsIr'de Hazards

Recent movement of the Salmon Falls landslide has created large open fractures
on the 4.5 ha area of the down-dropped canyon rim. This area probably also contains
open fractures that are bridged by soil and are thus not visible at the ground surface.
Some of these fractures likely reach depths of several tens of meters (Ellis et al., 2004).
The Luceme School basalt ﬂow that forms the upper canyon rim also contains numerous
open fractures associated with jointing planes and collapsed lava tubes. These fractures
have been widening as the headwall block has moved away from the canyon rim, making
the cliff face extremely unstable (Ellis et al., 2004).

Areas below the cliff, including the access trail to Bluegill Lake, are therefore
exposed to considerable risk from rock fall, as are areas of the cliff formerly used by

recreational rock climbers. Such hazards in the 4.5 ha area above the canyon rim affected

89

by the landslide and the areas along and below the canyon rim pose a signiﬁcant safety
threat to recreational visitors to the canyon.

5.2 Future Work and Other Investigations

5.2.1 Continued Monitoring

Monitoring of the Salmon Falls landslide is ongoing and future work will
continue to document the landslide’s motion. Ground-based total station surveying duties
will be resumed by the BLM with cooperation from ISU. If possible, survey prisms that
were destroyed and stolen by vandals will be replaced on the headwall block of the
landslide. Additional survey prisms could be installed throughout the slide and also to
the north and south of the observed displacement to better deﬁne the boundaries of active
landslide movement.

The autonomous GPS campaign ended in April, 2004. Future work may involve
acquiring additional GPS receivers that could be deployed throughout the slide and also
to the north and south of the observed displacement to help delineate the active part of the
landslide. Resumed GPS monitoring would also help to decipher the details of
precipitation and irrigation effects on the landslide motion. If funding permits, the GPS
system could be converted to a real-time system with cellular and internet alarm services.
5.2.2 LIDAR

Other studies of the Salmon Falls landslide by investigators at ISU have utilized
airborne LiDAR data, which was acquired in the fall 2002 over a ca.10 km2 area that
includes the landslide and surrounding canyon. The LiDAR data are evaluated for
surface roughness characteristics and ﬂood hazard modeling (Glenn et al., in prep). First

and last-pulse data along with intensities provide an opportunity to model landslide

90

surface characteristics. Surface roughness characteristics help in locating areas with
active movement within a slide and can be used to delineate the slide boundaries. Similar
studies utilizing this technique on active landslides have proven successful (McKean and
Roering, 2004).

The LiDAR data have also proven useful in ﬂood hazard modeling by providing
detailed digital elevation models (DEMs). Both downstream and upstream ﬂooding
scenarios were created and provided to the BLM for use in two ﬂood models (HEC-RAS,
a US Army Corps of Engineers model and FLDWAV, a National Weather Service
model). Downstream cross-sections (vertical and horizontal) of a ‘ﬂooded' canyon are
available with this dataset at as close as 1-m intervals. Upstream lake-levels were
projected with increased dam-building to model the canyon holding potential and wetting
surface.

5.2.3 SAR Interferometry

Other investigations of the Salmon Falls landslide at ISU have utilized SAR
interferometry in order to map topographic change of the landslide. Other studies of
landslide motion with InSAR (Bénédicte, 1996; Fruneau et al., 1996; Squarzoni et al.,
2003) have produced good results for very large landslides in areas where topography is
not very steep. The steep topography of Salmon Falls Creek Canyon has complicated the
InSAR study due to significant SAR image layover (Chadwick et al., 2003). Other
problems have been encountered due to the magnitude of movement of the slide in its
earlier stages of development, which may be too high on the majority of the slide’s
surface (estimated >10 m), leading to significant temporal decorrelation. Further

research will focus on the mitigation of these effects, which may prevent the use of

91

InSAR for topographic change mapping in all but the boundary areas of the slide where
less total movement has occurred. This portion of the study may therefore be most useful
for defining the boundaries of the active slide, which is less clear along the northern
boundary (Chadwick et al., 2003).

52.4 Geotechnicai Investigations

The drilling of exploratory borings and the installment of piezometers is needed to
determine the subsurface geology, material strengths, and groundwater interactions near
the landslide. A 150-200 m deep exploratory boring just east of the landslide headscarp
has been proposed by the USGS (Ellis et al., 2004) and ISU to determine the stratigraphy
in this part of the canyon, and to monitor long-term groundwater pressure variations that
likely play a critical role in maintaining landslide movement.

One of the major inhibitors in the current study is the lack of a deﬁned failure
surface and little knowledge about material strengths. Both of these components could be
addressed with borehole data. An inclirrometer could be installed to help deﬁne the
depth of the failure surface and undisturbed samples could be collected for material
strength testing. Data from such a boring would be critical to establish a better
understanding of the landslide and thus provide a basis for forecasting its long-term
behavior (Ellis et al., 2004).

5.3 Conclusions
5.3.1 Hypotheses Revisited

Hypothesis A: Remote sensing and ground—based measurements can be used to
determine the patterns and mechanisms of landslide motion.

This study has effectively utilized a variety of ﬁeld and remote sensing methods

to document the history and spatial patterns of movement of the active Salmon Falls

92

landslide. Surﬁcial movement and deformation patterns determined through fracture
mapping, image change detection, total station surveying, and GPS monitoring elucidate
the patterns and mechanisms of the slide’s motion. Surface deformation mapped through
GPS techniques identiﬁed contrasting deformational styles in three sectors of the
landslide. Surﬁcial movement monitored with a total station and GPS identiﬁed spatial
and temporal movement patterns of the landslide, while image change detection
techniques documented the pre-monitoring motion of the slide. These data have been
synthesized in order to determine the slide’s geometry and the mechanisms of motion.

Hypothesis B: The Salmon Falls landslide is a rotational—50116 failure.

Field reconnaissance and initial observations suggested that the Salmon Falls
landslide may be a rotational-style failure. However, based on the observed movement,
surface deformation, and ﬁeld observations the Salmon Falls landslide resembles a
wedge-style slide, with three elements that have horizontal and vertical translation (Figs.
23c and 24). The slide is moving as a semi-coherent mass with the headwall block
moving downward (dominantly) and westward, the body translating westward, with
minimal vertical motion, and the toe moving approximately equal amounts upward and
westward. This wedge—style model agrees with averaged velocity vectors from the total
station and GPS data and also with the observed surface deformation on the slide.

5.3.2 Long— Term Movement

The possibility remains that the Salmon Falls landslide may fail catastrophically.
Many slow moving landslides show periods of rest and periods with slow movements and
accelerations, leading sometimes to catastrophic failures (Gasparetto et al., 1996; Kilbum

and Petley, 2003). The GPS and theodolite data collected in this study show that the

93

Salmon Falls landslide is prone to significant increases and decreases in velocity with
time. Catastrophic slope failure is normally preceded by weeks to decades of
accelerating creep (V oight, 1978). The strength of materials in slow moving landslides is
gradually reduced by strain softening, and the slope may eventually fail in a catastrophic
way (Gasparetto et al., 1996). Another possibility is that stresses will slowly equalize
and create a more stable slope.

After a period of relatively rapid acceleration beginning in 1999 and continuing
into 2001, the Salmon Falls landslide is now moving at a slow rate with minor
ﬂuctuations in velocity. The current activity may eventually lead to catastrophic failure,
but this is difficult to predict. The slide may also continue to move at its current slow
rate, and eventually stabilize without a catastrophic failure ever occurring. Further
investigation and monitoring are needed to accurately predict the long-term behavior of
this landslide.

5. 3. 3 Srgnificance

This study represents an opportunity to study a poorly understood, slow-moving
landslide utilizing a variety of ﬁeld and remote sensing techniques. The relatively slow
speed at which this landslide is moving makes it an ideal natural laboratory to test ﬁeld-
based landslide monitoring techniques in conjunction with newly developed remote
sensing methods. High-resolution satellite imagery and high-precision GPS are relatively
young technologies and this study has contributed to the development of these tools for
landslide monitoring and hazard analysis.

This study is a good example of cooperation between academia and federal

government sectors in the study of a landslide hazard. New partnerships between

94

government agencies and academia to study landslide hazards are one of the major goals
of the National Landslide Hazards Mitigation Strategy, which was implemented by the
USGS in 2003 (Spiker and Gori, 2003). The strategy was developed in response to the
rising costs resulting from landslide hazards in the United States. The mission of the
landslide hazard mitigation strategy is to reduce the risk of loss of life, injuries, economic
costs, and destruction of natural and cultural resources that result from landslides and
other mass wasting hazards.

Studies of the Salmon Falls landslide and associated hazards were enhanced by
the cooperation between ISU, the USGS and BLM. The collaboration between
researchers from these groups will continue in order to develop a more complete
understanding of this landslide hazard. This research project adds to the comprehensive
understanding of landslide processes and mechanisms as part of the growing effort to
advance our ability to forecast the behavior of differing types of landslides and keep

potential socioeconomic losses due to landslides at a minimum.
